STAT 310: Intermediate Statistics

Textbook

There is no required text for this class. A recommended text for student reference is Stat 2: Building Models for a World of Data by Cannon et al. This text is well-written and has some nice problems for additional student practice. However, it can get technical at times. You will not need this text to complete the homework or succeed on exams.

Prerequisites

Stat 110, STAT 210, STAT 303, STAT 305, PSY231, or equivalent.

Technology

We will make extensive use of JMP to perform most statistical analyses in this course. Instructions for downloading JMP onto your laptop are on the course webpage.

Learning Outcomes

STAT 310 will reinforce topics from your first course in statistics, including the logic of inference, and the proper scope of conclusions drawn from research studies. We will also learn about the concept of a statistical model. This course will promote your abilities to…

  • identify research questions that may be answered using various statistical models and to translate these questions into the appropriate analysis procedure. A successful student will be a knowledgeable consumer of the statistical methods presented, and be able to properly obtain and use these methods.

  • model and solve real-world problems, as well as understand limitations of models making predictions and drawing conclusions. Almost all the problems we will encounter are real-world examples. A successful student will be able to answer research questions with statistical models and to approach such problems with confidence.

  • recognize underlying model assumptions and employ methods for checking these assumptions.

  • analyze data and summarize the results in a meaningful way. You should have acquired some of these skills in your introductory statistics course, but in this class you will focus even more on effectively communicating results.

  • use appropriate technology to carry out analyses.

Assessment tasks

The following assessments will be used to gauge growth in the above learning outcomes.

Participation (0%)

Although you will not be graded on participation, attending class is essential to succeed in this course. Class time will often be spent completing group activities that mimic homework assignments. If you are not in class, you are missing out these valuable learning experiences that will help you do well on your homework and on exams.

Homework (35%)

Homework will be assigned regularly throughout the semester. These assignments will almost always involve the use of JMP to analyze real data and will require you to communicate your results in clearly written interpretations and conclusions. You are encouraged to work together on your homework, but each student must turn in their own original copy with answers in their own words. Late homework will be penalized, and any homework submitted after the key is posted will receive a 0. Aim to get your homework done 24 hours before it is due to minimize the chance of unforeseen circumstances delaying its completion.

Two midterm exams (20% each)

Midterm exam dates will be announced at least one week in advance. The exams will require you to apply what you learn from homework and lecture to accurately interpret data summaries and statistical output. Make-up exams will only be given in cases of extreme personal or family emergency. I must be notified in advance of the exam date if you wish to schedule a make-up exam; failure to comply may result in a grade of 0 on that exam.

Final exam (25%)

Final exam dates are set by the university. This exam will require you to apply what you learn from homework and lecture to accurately interpret data summaries and statistical output. Make-up exams will only be given in cases of extreme personal or family emergency. I must be notified in advance of the exam date if you wish to schedule a make-up exam; failure to comply may result in a grade of 0 on that exam.

Writing intensive

Stat 310 satisfies the GEP Writing Intensive. To qualify as a writing intensive, students must:

  • practice the processes and procedures for creating and completing successful writing in their fields;
  • understand the main features and uses of writing in their fields;
  • adapt their writing to the general expectations of readers in their field;
  • make use of technologies commonly used for research and writing in their fields;
  • learn the conventions of evidence, format, usage, and documentation in their fields.

A substantial portion of each exam will be take-home, and you will be graded according to a writing rubric on each of these take-home portions.

Tentative outline of topics

I. Review of statistical concepts

a. Definitions
b. Hypothesis testing and p-values
c. Binomial exact test
d. 2-sample t-test

II. Statistical power

a. Type-I and Type-II Error
b. What affects power?
c. Power calculations
d. Effect sizes

III. Introduction to modeling

a. The $Data = Model + Error$ framework
b. Different models for different data types
c. Viewing the t-test through the lens of a model

IV. Linear regression

a. Simple linear regression
b. Different intercepts: controlling for a binary variable
c. Different slopes: interactions with a binary variable

V. ANOVA

a. One-way ANOVA
b. Adjusting for multiple comparisons
c. Two-way ANOVA: Main effects
d. Two-way ANOVA: Interaction

VI. Design strategies

a. Randomization
b. Blocking
c. Factorial designs

VII. Logistic regression

a. Introduction to the odds ratio
b. Simple logistic regression model
c. Multiple logistic regression
