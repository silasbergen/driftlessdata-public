Stat 110 Syllabus (Summer 2019)

May 6, 2019

Required software

  • JMP - available from WSU network for free, or via email (if not using WSU laptop). Download instructions for WSU laptops

Optional text

There is no text required for this course. However, if you would like to refer to a textbook, a good reference is Unlocking the Power of Data by Lock, Lock, Lock, Lock, and Lock.

Prerequisites

Qualifying score on the mathematics placement exam or MATH 050.

Office hours

I will hold office hours on-demand (set up appointment via email). I can hold these in person if you are in Winona, or virtually via an Adobe Connect meeting room. If office hours are via Adobe Connect, I will record the session so that other students can view the office hours.

D2L Discussion board

Ask any content-related questions via the D2L discussion board. If you email me a question that I think would be useful for the entire class, I will ask you to re-post it on the Discussion board so everyone can benefit from the answer.

Email policy

I will try to check and respond to my email at least once per day during the week. Do not expect me to use my email like an instant chat. There may be some days where I am unable to check email, and I will notify you in advance of these dates if this is the case.

Note on lecture video quizzes

The lecture power points are “gap style,” meaning that they contain many questions with blank answers. Part of your note-taking process is to fill in your lecture notes as you watch the lecture videos. Students often find it helpful to print out the powerpoints to take notes while they watch the videos. These lecture videos are also “quizzes,” and pause periodically to ask quiz questions. These questions are not tracked as part of your grade, but merely intended to keep you engaged and thinking about the material as it is presented.

Weekly Schedule

I will post the weekly schedule on the course website. In a typical week, there are five sets of tasks; of which two are for your grade:

  1. Watch lecture videos
  2. Take practice D2L quizzes
  3. Complete posted practice problems, and check your work with the key
  4. Complete GRADED D2L quiz (typically due Thursday end of day)
  5. Complete GRADED homework and upload to D2L (typically due Monday end of day)

I will also post a checklist in D2L for each week that you can optionally use to keep track of your to-do list.

This is a heavy weekly load, and you will be busy! I hope you are not taking an online summer course because you think it will be easier than a regular-semester version! You are taking a course that is typically offered over a 15-week semester in 7 weeks, less than half the time! Accordingly, you will need to be especially careful to keep up with watching videos, doing practice quizzes, attempting practice problems, taking graded quizzes, and completing homework. Allow yourself plenty of time! Make use of the D2L checklist to track your tasks and due dates!

Practice items

These items will not be graded, but are intended to give you additional practice in order to do well on the graded quizzes and homework.

Practice quizzes I will post practice quizzes in D2L that you can take an unlimited number of times for additional practice before taking your graded quiz. Availability of these quizzes ends on the day your graded quiz is due.

Practice problems These longer-form problems will more closely mimic the homework assignments. I will post a key for each practice problem assignment, however I strongly encourage you to attempt the practice problems on your own before viewing the key!

Graded assessment items

The following assessments will be used to calculate student grades.

Quizzes (30%)

Each week, you will take a quiz in D2L on the lecture content for that week. Each quiz will be timed and must be completed in the allotted time frame.

The quizzes will mainly consist of multiple choice and True/False questions, with occasional short-answer questions.

Homework assignments (30%)

Whereas the quizzes are multiple choice and T/F, written homework assignments require deeper thinking and will include written responses, often accompanied by computer output. These will be distributed in Word documents, and you will type your responses in these documents and upload your submission to D2L. Late homework is subject to penalty. Homework that is submitted after I have uploaded a key will receive a 0.

Midterm exam (20%)

Final exam (20%)

Each exam will be taken in D2L. The exams will require you to demonstrate your understanding of the material as opposed to your ability to memorize certain concepts. The format will primarily be a combination of short answer, multiple choice, and T/F. The exams will be timed and must be completed in the allotted time frame.

I will provide more detail on the exams as the time draws near, as well as provide practice exams.

Final grades

Final grades will be a percent that accounts for work on quizzes, homework and exams. Final grades will be assigned as follows:

  • 90-100%: A
  • 80-89%: Guaranteed at least a B
  • 70-79%: Guaranteed at least a C
  • 60-69%: Guaranteed at least a D
  • <60%: No lower than F

Academic integrity policy

All students must abide by Winona State University’s Academic Integrity Policy. The first occurrence of violating the academic integrity policy will result in a significant loss of points on that specific homework assignment or exam; the second occurrence will result in failure of the course. The following constitute violations of academic integrity:

  • copying another student’s work
  • allowing someone to copy your work
  • emailing your work to someone
  • assisting someone with graded weekly quizzes
  • receiving assistance on graded weekly quizzes

Tentative course outline

  1. Introduction to data

  2. Descriptive Methods for a Single Variable

    a. Barplots, counts, percents

    b. Histograms, mean/median, quantiles, standard deviation

  3. Describing associations between 2 variables

    a. Mosaic plots

    b. Boxplots

    c. Scatterplots

  4. Multivariable thinking

    a. Confounding

    b. Confounding adjustment

  5. Introduction to inferential thinking

    a. Introduction to Statistical Thinking

    b. Some Definitions

    c. The Importance of Random Sampling

  6. Methods for a Single Categorical Variable

    a. Descriptive Methods

    b. Discussion of Sample-to-Sample Variation

    c. Using the Binomial Probability Model to Make Decisions

    d. Formal Statistical Inference (Binomial Exact Test, Confidence Interval for a Single Proportion, Chi-Square Goodness of Fit Test)

  7. Methods for Two or More Categorical Variables

    a. Descriptive Methods

    b. More Descriptive Methods: Relative Risk and Odds Ratios

    c. Inferences for Comparing Two Proportions (Chi-Square Test, and Confidence Interval for a Difference in or ratio of Proportions)

    d. Understanding the Basics of Experimental Design and Correlation vs. Causation

    e. Inferences for Comparing Several Proportions Across Another Variable (Chi-Square Test)

  8. Inferential Methods for a Single Numerical Variable

    a. Inference for a Single Mean (t-test and confidence interval for a single mean)

    b. Inference for Comparing Two Means with Dependent Groups (paired t-test, confidence interval for a mean difference)

    c. Inference for Comparing Two Means with Independent Groups (t-test for independent samples, confidence interval for a difference in means)

    d. Inference for Comparing Several Group Means (ANOVA)

  9. Methods for Two or More Numerical Variables

    a. Descriptive Methods (correlation, scatterplots)

    b. Introduction to Simple Linear Regression
